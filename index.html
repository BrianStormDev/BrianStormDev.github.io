Being an Ethical Engineer/Student

Written by Brian Leong

If you haven't heard this yet, welcome to UC Berkeley! CS61A is one of the first courses that students take when entering the field of Computer Science. But whether you plan on pursuing Computer Science as a career or are simply interested in Computer Science, we want to inform you of some important ethical considerations you should take. Note that while this is framed from the perspective of preparing oncoming engineers and computer scientists, you may still find this relevant for your future prospects.

This list is not complete and is subject to change and be updated. If you find there are additions to be made, feel free to email cs61a@berkeley.edu or myself at bleong314@berkeley.edu!

Why are We Mentioning This?

Whether it be during your time here in CS61A or some time later in life, we recognize that you will be presented with many opportunities to contribute to emerging technologies. Considering that we are an introductory class, we want to introduce you to the reality that you will be facing decisions that impact people. While it might be easy to think that you are just typing lines on a computer, many programs have the power to change the world for the better or for the worse. Additionally, how you treat others in this course will also shape how you are perceived by others both now and in the future.

At UC Berkeley, we emphasize the Honor Code because we believe that every student should treat others with kindness and respect. While you may be convinced that you only have to uphold such codes while you are here at UC Berkeley, the truth is that these ethical principles apply in the workforce and in any sort of occupation that you might be preparing for in the future.

Your time here at UC Berkeley is very similar to what you'll experience in the workplace. You'll be faced with deadlines, pressure to perform, and will be working with others. You may be tempted on many occasions to take shortcuts, but we encourage you to avoid cheating. By cheating, you both undermine the efforts of other students, and hinder your own learning. When you are in the workforce, you will be solving problems that no one has ever solved before! So while you are here, you should prepare yourself by upholding academic honesty. By working on your ethical habits during your time here in CS61A, you'll be more than prepared for when you enter the workforce. But more importantly, you'll work towards becoming a better person.

Being Ethical Regarding your Own Work

Here we mention some ethical considerations regarding your own work:

  1. Academic Integrity: As a student as UC Berkeley, we want to prepare you to be your best when you join the workforce. An important part of that preparation comes with academic integrity. Part of the reason why we enforce academic integrity is because we want to ensure you get the most out of the class. That means that behavior such as copying other students' code or asking an online forum for answers is unacceptable, and is subject to academic penalties. If you ever find yourself tempted to cheat, remind yourself that you risk not only those penalities, but are undermining your own development in Computer Science!
  2. The Use of Artificial Intelligence: Artificial Intelligence has become more and more prevalent in both industry and student workflows. While it can be used to support your learning, it should not be used as a replacement. What makes a good and ethical Computer Scientist is one who can solve problems on their own without simply pasting AI content for credit. After all, the people who are hiring are not looking for a prompt engineer, but rather someone who can solve problems!

Being Ethical When Working with Others

Here we provide a few gudelines on how be ethical when working on a team:

  1. Never Condescend: The staff here at CS61A strongly believe that everyone is capable of learning. People often excel in different areas, after all, that is why people major in different subjects at college! But often you don't get to experience that gap until you work in a group or partner setting. In this class, you will often have to work in a group, and it may be tempting to talk poorly about others who are not doing as well on a subject or do not seem as focused as you are. However, it's important to recognize that condescending doesn't benefit anyone. The next time you are tempted to talk poorly about someone, try framing it as constructive criticism, rather than an attack on one's character.
  2. Share the Credit: Related to the previous point, take time to recognize the people who are working with you and what they are contributing. Often we only consider our own contributions rather than the contributions of others. Even if it feels like you are the one who is doing all of the coding (which shouldn't be the case if you are doing pair programming on course projects!), know that everyone learns at a different pace. So even if your partner or group may not be meeting your expectations, try to recognize when they are working hard.
  3. Respect the Staff: You'll find that in many workplaces, you will often have superiors who are in charge of managing you and your coworkers. Sometimes, you may not get along with your superiors, but that doesn't mean that you should insult or ignore them. Often there are a lot of things happening in the life of course staff members and by not treating them with respect, you discourage them from further contributing to Computer Science education.

Being Ethical With What You Work On

Here we provide a few guidelines on how be ethical when deciding who to work for or what to work on.

  1. Ensure you align with Employer Values: As a student as UC Berkeley, you will presented with many opportunities for places to work, whether that be research or a job. However, one must recognize how a role's ethics lines up with their own. Sometimes it's easy to let big salary numbers entice individuals to take jobs, however, make sure to do your research on who you are working for.
  2. Always Test Your Code: Engineering is an ongoing process, and it’s critical to adopt the habit of consistently testing your work. In courses like CS61B, where tests are not predefined for you, you'll encounter a similar challenge in the real world. It’s your responsibility to ensure the accuracy and safety of your code, as it can have serious implications for others' lives. An example of this is the case of Therac-25: A machine that was programmed to give radiation therapy to patients in need. However, due to a number of failures in programming the needed safety mechanisms, the machine ended up overdosing patients with radiation on multiple occasions, resulting in their deaths. It's important to keep this emphasis in mind early on in your career, as it may prevent critical accidents from occurring.

Speaking Up / Reporting an Incident

We recognize that disputes and conflicts can occur between students, staff, instructors, etc. There are many different types of incidents, including but not limited to discrimination, assault, conflict, etc... Nevertheless, we want to encourage you to speak up if you notice anything suspicious. Thus, we provide many resources where you can anonymously report any negative experiences.

  • The EECS department provides many ways to report issues within the department, including general incidents, grievances with faculty, sexual harassment, and discrimination. Please report any incidents to us or to the department using the department form, so that we can continue to improve the CS student experience.
  • The Student Advocate’s Office (SAO) "is a student-run organization that helps students litigate issues with professors."
  • The Ombudsperson for Students "can be your first step, your last resort, or anything in between. The Ombudsperson will listen to your concerns, serve as a sounding board, discuss your options with you, and help you get a new perspective and determine the next steps to take."
  • Lastly, many resources of support and ways to report issues are provided by the university as well.

Want to Learn More?

If you are interested in learning more about ethics, or fine tuning your own personal ethical code, consider taking the following classes at UC Berkeley!

  • ENGIN-125 (Ethics, Engineering, and Society): Provides an introduction to how theories, concepts, and methods from the humanities and social science can be applied to ethical dilemmas you may face in engineering. You'll get the chance to discuss emerging engineering technologies with fellow students and reflect on your own ethical values.
  • CS-195 (Social Implications of Computer Technology): Dives into the ethical concerns of emerging computer technologies. Discusses ideas such as intellectual property, data privacy, artificial intelligence, pornography and censorship, etc... The course also teaches about professional ethics that will be vital for students entering the workforce.
  • BIOE-100 (Ethics in Science and Engineering): Discusses issues of professional conduct in the practice of engineering, research, publication, public and private disclosures. Mentions various case studies and debates on a variety of ethical issues
